Supernova remnants

 

Supernova remnants are the result of the interaction between ejected material from a supernova explosion and the ambient medium surrounding the progenitor. I have been involved in four different projects concerning supernova remnants:   


  1. • The morphology of supernova remnants in The Small Magellanic Cloud

  2. • The origin of the explosion mechanism of the Galactic supernova remnant, W49B

  3. • The overionization features in the supernova remnant, W49B

  4. • The spatial distribution of overionization and molecular material in mixed morphology supernova remnants

W49B is a young supernova remnant located in our own Galaxy. As we see it from Earth, a 1.000 years have passed, since the supernova originally exploded. Our main results of the study of this remnant are that:


  1. -The explosion was most likely a bipolar/jet-driven Type Ib/Ic supernova

- A black hole was formed at the center of the remnant

  during the explosion

- The iron is highly segregated from the other elements

- The plasma conditions vary throughout the remnant

  1. -We see overionization features in the remnant, which

   is unusual in a young supernova remnant

What is overionization?

That a plasma is overionized means that the ions in the plasma are stripped of more electrons, than one would expect at the given plasma temperature, if the plasma had been in collisional ionization equilibrium (CIE). That W49B appears to be overly ionized is interesting, since young supernova remnants are actually predicted by theory to be underionized. Hence, the ions are normally expected to be stripped of less electrons than one would expect at the given plasma temperature, due to the long timescales for collisional ionization compared to the heating timescale.
